The Challenges of Mobile Advertising

Jan
23

2015

Mobile is an exploding media and mobile advertising is following our collective eyeballs and attention; already worth $3.5 Billion in the US and $18 Billion globally, its compound annual growth is projected to be an astounding 49.7% through 2018 (the next fastest growing categories are Social and Desktop Video at 18% and 15% respectively.)

And yet although mobile as a share of our overall media consumption already accounts for 25%, mobile advertising as a share of advertising stills lags significantly, at 9.8%.

This could be explained as simply a “lag” as mobile advertising “catches up” with mobile media consumption. However, if we look at other data points such as a comparison of mobile commerce to e-commerce we see that mobile is only 1/3 as effective as a transaction platform.

Mobile is expected to be more important as an “influencer” of retail sales, for example through show-rooming, iBeacon and similar location based services. Only 2.4% of US retail transactions in the US took place on a mobile device in 2014 (e-Marketer), but 19% of all US retail transactions were “influenced” by mobile devices in 2014 (Deloitte).

In addition, technologies such as ApplePay are expected to empower mobile devices as stand alone transaction vehicles.

So maybe the lower conversion effectiveness of m-commerce so far is not an issue?

Another data point, Google’s average mobile CPC (Cost Per Click) shows it decreasing at a very rapid pace (10% Q4 2013 and 10% Q1 2014). Google no longer breaks down CPC by mobile, but the fact that overall CPC for Google is going down while mobile share goes up is a clear indication of the challenges Google faces in mobile advertising.

One of the most profitable and biggest growth drivers in mobile advertising so far are app downloads (350 million app downloads by Facebook alone between June 2012 and Q1 2014). However, average app CPI (Cost Per Install) rates are increasing ($1 to $2.39 for Android and iOS respectively in 2014) as the conversion efficiency of the CPC led campaigns decreases.

More importantly, app churn rates are huge and growing, with some analysts claiming an average 8% monthly churn in the 1st year, implying only 4% of users are retained within 12 months of download.

This is not surprising, given the limited real estate on mobile devices and no more than 30 installed apps on our devices on average.

Why is mobile so challenging for the traditional advertising model?

Screen size is a factor of course. That said, native formats such as the in-stream ads served by Facebook actually imply a larger average size of Facebook mobile ad units versus ad formats used in desktop display advertising.

An app centric mobile ecosystem, versus the search centric universe of desktop browsing can also help explain some of the challenges, in particular those of Google.

But the single most important difference, the one that truly makes “Mobile Advertising” a category that requires a re-invention of the advertising model that has existed over the last 130 years, is the average session length on a mobile device.

When we sit down to enjoy a movie, read a newspaper, listen to the radio or interact with our desktop, chances are we will be engaged with any of those media for at significant periods of time.

Whereas although we engage with our smartphones often, and spend on aggregate a significant amount of time on them the typical amount of time we spend interacting on a single session on a smartphone (checking email, messages, weather, stocks, news, etc.) is very short.

And thus our tolerance for being interrupted by an advert during a short mobile session is vastly reduced.

This radically upends the traditional advertising model Quid Pro Quo, Free or Subsidized Content + Advertising = Conversion and Uplift.

Where do we go from here? The Good, the Bad and the Ugly Sales process

Most of us would not start a sales process by bragging to our potential customer about how great our product is, what its features are, how much better or cheaper than the competition it is, etc.

Instead, when we do a really good job of selling, we start with our customer. We ask them questions, about their problems, their challenges, their routines. We engage them in a two way conversation about their issues. It’s about them, not us.

And yet advertising has never worked that way. It has mostly been a one directional medium of messaging potential customers, without starting our interaction the way a good salesperson would; by asking them questions and getting them engaged.

That's because, until recently, it simply was not possible to do this. The technology and infrastructure did not exist to support this. But now it does.

But now it is possible to Gamify the Advertising Experience and enable a two way interaction, a true conversation, focused on the needs and wants of the customer speaking about the features and capabilities we offer.

We call this approach “Mobile Quest Marketing”. It allows for triggering an experiential engagement of targeted prospects at offline or online points of sale.

By leveraging the latest mobile and adtech technologies including location based services such as iBeacon, as well as the latest insights of behavioural science it is now possible to target the right persons at the right place in the right context, and trigger an experiential engagement that focuses on their interests in a fun and rewarded set of activities.

This type of service allows us to sell CPA (Cost Per Action) performance based marketing. In other words, whereas many digital platforms to date have focused on selling leads (CPM, CPC, CPI), we as an industry can now focus on selling customers (CPA).

Using the new system we have already delivered 10X reductions in cost of customer acquisition through our advertising gamification platform, and a 20X improvement in engagement.

A huge advance that will completely refashion the advertising industry.
